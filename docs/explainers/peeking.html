Peeking: how checking your test daily manufactures false positives

A growth PM launches a signup test on a short-video app, watches the dashboard every morning, and plans to call it the first day the p-value goes green. The feature does nothing. Here is how often that habit declares victory anyway.

Every number below comes from 4,000 simulated experiments with the true effect fixed at exactly zero. Reproduce this figure.

What 5% is supposed to mean

Test once at alpha = 0.05 and, when the feature truly does nothing, the test comes back "significant" in only 5% of experiments. The dashed line marks that 5%. It is the error rate the team believes it is running, and it holds for exactly one look at the data.

Check daily, stop at green

Now the PM checks every morning and stops the first day p < 0.05. The red curve is the share of the 4,000 no-effect experiments already called "significant" by each day. By day 14 it reaches 24.1%: five times the error rate the team believes it is running.

Why it inflates

Each look is another draw from the noise. The daily z-statistics are correlated, so it is not 14 independent 5% chances, but the maximum of 14 correlated tests still crosses 1.96 far more often than one test does. More looks, more chances; the curve only ever goes up.

Price the looks in advance

The fix is not "stop peeking", it is peeking with a budget. A Lan-DeMets alpha-spending boundary raises the bar for early looks: day one demands |z| > 8.03, day seven 3.01, day fourteen 2.10, so the whole 14-look family spends exactly 0.05. Early stops are still allowed; they just require early-stop evidence.

Same data, honest error rate

Apply that boundary to the same 4,000 experiments: the daily-peek false positive rate comes back to 4.95%, on the promise. And when a real effect exists, the boundary still stops early, so the cost of honesty is small.

The memo you write instead

Recommendation

Keep the daily dashboard, but pre-commit an O'Brien-Fleming alpha-spending boundary for the daily looks; do not stop on a raw p < 0.05. Under daily uncorrected checks, 24.1% of null experiments would ship a false winner (measured over 4,000 simulated runs); the boundary restores the nominal 5% while preserving early stopping for real effects.

Method note: the boundary is computed with the Lan-DeMets alpha-spending approach (Lan and DeMets, "Discrete sequential boundaries for clinical trials", Biometrika 70(3), 1983) using an O'Brien-Fleming-type spending function, and the implementation is validated against published reference values in tests/unit/test_sequential.py.
