When you cannot randomize

The feature launched city by city, big cities first, months ago. Leadership wants the impact number anyway. This is the analysis you run when the experiment never happened, and the check that decides whether to believe it.

Simulated city-by-period panel with every component explicit: city effects, a common trend, the launch effect (2.0 by construction), and optionally a differential trend. Reproduce this figure.

The groups were never comparable

Launched cities sit well above unlaunched ones before the launch even happens, because adoption followed size. Both drift upward on a common trend. Nothing here is randomized; every difference is entangled with why cities launched when they did.

Two obvious numbers, both wrong

After-minus-before in launched cities: 5.91, because it absorbs the trend everyone was riding. Launched-versus-unlaunched after launch: 5.32, because it absorbs the pre-existing gap. The constructed truth is 2.0. Both numbers would be presented with straight faces in a real review.

Difference the differences

Take each group's change from before to after, then subtract: the city gap cancels, the common trend cancels, and what remains is 1.95, on the truth. That is difference-in-differences, and it rests on exactly one assumption: without the launch, the two groups would have moved in parallel.

The assumption fails quietly

Now the same panel, but launched cities were already growing faster (a differential trend of 0.3 per period; think: the cities chosen first were chosen because they were taking off). DiD reads 4.36 against the same truth of 2.0, a 118% overstatement, and nothing in the post-launch data warns you. DiD does not fail loudly; it fails fluently.

The placebo test is the price of admission

Estimate the "effect" of a fake launch placed in the middle of the pre-period, where the true effect is zero by construction. Parallel world: -0.13, boring, as it should be. Divergent world: 1.07, alarm. A DiD readout without a pre-trend placebo is an assumption wearing a confidence interval.

The memo you write instead

Recommendation

Report the difference-in-differences estimate only alongside its placebo: on this panel the pre-period placebo reads 1.07 where zero is expected, so the parallel-trends assumption fails and the headline DiD (4.36) overstates the true effect (2.0 by construction) by more than double. Either match launched cities to controls on pre-period growth before differencing, or state the number as an upper bound, not an estimate.

The pattern generalizes: every quasi-experimental method is an untestable assumption plus arithmetic. The placebo does not prove the assumption; it is the cheapest way to catch it failing. When the decision is worth real money and the placebo fires, the honest answer is a real experiment, even a small one.
