Sample ratio mismatch: the smoke alarm

Your A/B test assigned users 50/50. The logged data came back 49.5/50.5. That half-percent is the most informative number in the readout, and the correct response is not a correction. It is an investigation.

Simulated experiment with 100,000 users per arm and a logging bug that silently drops 2% of one arm, non-randomly. Reproduce this figure.

The eye cannot see it

Treatment logged 98,022 users, control 100,000. The bars look identical; a 1% gap at this scale is invisible on any dashboard. In this simulation the cause is planted: treatment users on old app versions crash before exposure is logged.

The chi-square can

A goodness-of-fit test of the logged counts against the designed split gives p = 0.0000088. Randomization makes the split one of the few things the experimenter knows exactly, so even small deviations are loud evidence that something between assignment and logging is broken.

Why it voids the readout

The missing users are not random: crashes concentrate among low-engagement users, so the treatment arm quietly sheds its low end. The estimate reads 0.53 against a constructed truth of 0.50, and the bias matches the closed form computed from who was dropped. With the mechanism unknown in real life, no reweighting is trustworthy.

What the alarm can and cannot hear

Detection is sampling arithmetic: the alarm fires once the missing users outgrow the split's noise, roughly 3.3 x sqrt(total) users at the conventional alpha of 0.001. At 100k per arm a 2% one-arm loss is caught essentially always and a 1% loss usually is not; at 1M per arm the same 1% is caught essentially always. Small experiments do not get this protection for small bugs.

Alarm means stop

An SRM is a symptom, not a statistic to adjust (Fabijan et al. catalog the causes: assignment bugs, logging loss, bot filtering, redirects). The operating rule at every mature experimentation platform: run the check on every experiment, and when it fires, stop the analysis and find the bug. The p-value on the metric is void until the p-value on the counts is boring.

The memo you write instead

Recommendation

Do not read the metric results; the assignment counts fail the SRM check (p = 0.0000088 against the designed 50/50). In this simulated case the surviving data overstates the true effect (0.53 measured against a constructed 0.50) because the loss concentrates among low-engagement treatment users. Find and fix the logging path, then rerun; do not reweight around an unknown missingness mechanism.

Method note: Fabijan et al., "Diagnosing Sample Ratio Mismatch in Online Controlled Experiments: A Taxonomy and Rules of Thumb for Practitioners", KDD 2019. The check is validated against scipy.stats.chisquare in tests.
