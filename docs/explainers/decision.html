From p-value to decision

A short-video app redesigns its feed layout. Week one looks like a triumph. This page runs the readout end to end, the way the launch review should: what the number is, what broke it, and what to recommend.

One simulated experiment: long-run effect 0.10, novelty spike 0.40 decaying over days, 20,000 users per arm. Reproduce this figure.

The effect is a path, not a point

A visible redesign gets attention that fades. In this simulation the truth is set explicitly: a persistent effect of 0.10 plus a novelty spike of 0.40 decaying with a 5-day time constant. The launch decision is about the plateau, because the plateau is what the company keeps.

What the impatient readout says

Stop after week one and the estimate is 0.32, highly significant, and mostly novelty: a 3.2x overstatement of the long-run effect. The p-value is not wrong; the estimate is real. It is an accurate measurement of a temporary thing.

What the patient readout says

Run four weeks and read the last seven days: 0.13, sitting near the true plateau of 0.10. Even the full four-week average (0.19) still carries the spike. The estimator that answers the launch question is the post-burn-in window, and the runtime exists to make that window possible.

The memo carries the decision, not the p-value

A readout is finished when someone who will not look at the chart can act on it. The format that survives contact with a launch review: recommendation first, effect in business units with its interval, the validity threats checked and quantified, and what evidence would change the answer. The full example is below.

The memo, in full

Recommendation

Ship the redesign, and set expectations at the plateau: a sustained lift of about 0.13 units per user (post-burn-in window, days 22 to 28), not the 0.32 the week-one readout suggested, which was mostly novelty (measured against a constructed truth of 0.10 long-run plus a decaying spike).

Threats checked

Novelty: the week-1 estimate overstates the long-run effect 3.2x; decision taken on the last-week window instead. Peeking: readout points were pre-committed (day 14, day 28). Power: sized for the plateau effect, not the spike.

What would change the answer

If the last-week estimate had kept falling week over week rather than flattening, ship would become re-test with a longer horizon; flat weeks 3 and 4 are what make the plateau claim credible.
