Power, MDE, and how long to run

"Let's run it two weeks and see" is a sample size decision made by a calendar. The arithmetic takes five minutes and tells you before launch whether the experiment can answer its own question.

Power is a curve, not a vibe

Fix the lift you care about, a 1-point lift on a 10% signup rate, and power is a function of sample size alone. It crosses the standard 80% bar at about 14,000 users per arm. Below that, a real effect more often than not fails to reach significance; the experiment is designed to shrug.

Halve the effect, quadruple the bill

Required sample scales with one over the effect squared. Half the lift (0.5 points) needs about 56,000 per arm, four times as much; double the lift (2 points) needs about 3,500. This is why "we'll detect whatever is there" is not a plan: whatever is there has a price, and small effects are expensive.

Runtime is the same curve in disguise

At 2,500 new users per arm per day, the minimum detectable effect falls with the square root of runtime: after one week the experiment can see a 0.90-point lift, after four weeks 0.45. The first week buys the most sensitivity; every week after buys less than the one before.

Decide the effect first

The business question comes first: what is the smallest lift worth shipping and maintaining? That number plus the traffic sets the runtime, before launch, on paper. If the runtime comes out at a quarter, the answer is not to run it two weeks anyway; it is a bigger treatment, a more sensitive metric, or CUPED, which buys back sample size for free. And the runtime must clear the novelty window, not just the power bar.

The memo you write instead

Recommendation

Size the test to the smallest lift worth shipping: for a 1-point lift on the 10% signup rate, that is roughly 14,000 users per arm (5.6 days at current traffic); do not launch the 0.5-point version without 56,000 per arm or a variance-reduction plan.
