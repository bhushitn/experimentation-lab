Choosing the right test

Welch's t, the two-proportion z, Mann-Whitney U. The folklore says "ugly distribution, use the rank test." The folklore costs you a third of your power on exactly the metric it is invoked for.

Distributions and powers measured on the engine's simulated metrics; 200 experiments of 20,000 users per arm for the power comparison. Reproduce this figure.

When the mean behaves

Engagement-style metrics: roughly symmetric, tails under control. Welch's t-test on the difference in means is the default and the right one; it does not assume equal variances, and at experiment sample sizes the central limit theorem does the rest. Binary metrics are the same story with less variance bookkeeping: the two-proportion z-test.

When it does not

Revenue-shaped metrics: 70% of users are exact zeros and the rest are drawn from a heavy right tail. The histogram looks nothing like a bell, and the reflex is to reach for Mann-Whitney because "the t-test needs normality." The t-test needs normal means, and with 20,000 users per arm it has them.

The folklore, measured

Give treatment a real effect: active users spend 25% more, activity unchanged. On 200 simulated experiments, Welch's t detects it in 100% of runs; Mann-Whitney in 68%. The zeros are identical in both arms, and identical values carry no rank information, so the rank test throws away the 70% of the sample where nothing moved and under-weights how much the spenders moved.

Ask what the decision needs

Launch decisions are usually about totals: revenue per user, minutes per user. Those are means, so test means, and use the t-test's fragility budget where it belongs: run CUPED to cut variance, or a bootstrap CI when n is genuinely small. Mann-Whitney answers a different question ("does treatment shift the distribution?"): a fine robustness check, a wrong drop-in replacement.

The memo you write instead

Recommendation

Decide on mean revenue per user with Welch's t-test and report Mann-Whitney only as a robustness footnote. On this metric shape (70% zeros, heavy tail) with a spend-scaling effect, the rank test's measured power is 68% against the t-test's 100% (200 simulated experiments, 20,000 users per arm), so making it the primary test risks missing a real win a third of the time.
